The construction and simulation of anatomically and biophysically detailed models is becoming a standard tool in neuroscience (Einevoll et al., 2019). Such models, which typically employ the compartmental modeling approach and a Hodgkin-Huxley-type description of voltage-gated ion channels, are thought to be capable of providing fairly accurate models of single neurons (Bower, 2015; Hay, Hill, Schürmann, Markram, & Segev, 2011; Herz, Gollisch, Machens, & Jaeger, 2006; Káli & Freund, 2005; R. Migliore et al., 2018; Poirazi, Brannon, & Mel, 2003b; but see Almog & Korngreen, 2014) and (when complemented by appropriate models of synaptic interactions) even large-scale circuits (Markram et al., 2015). However, building such detailed multi-compartmental models of neurons requires setting a large number of parameters (such as the densities of various ion channels in multiple neuronal compartments) that are often not directly constrained by the available experimental data. These parameters are typically tuned (either manually or using automated parameter-search methods (Almog & Korngreen, 2014; Friedrich, Vella, Gulyás, Freund, & Káli, 2014; van Geit et al., 2016) until the simulated physiological behavior of the model matches some pre-defined set of experimental observations.
